Plotting territorial income distribution and other demographic indicators in the city of Barcelona


The objective of this article is to analyze and plot choropleth maps of territorial income distribution and other demographic indicators in the city of Barcelona obtained from Barcelona open data repository using Python's Folium library. Here we only present the final results, detailed technical implementation explaination is available in my Jupyter Notebook, and the source code is available in my Github repository.

1. Income distribution in the city of Barcelona

The first dataset that we are going to analyze is the Renta Familiar Disponible (RFD) index dataset, this dataset contains the Disposable Family Income Index by Neighborhood, the most recent one is from year 2017.

The RFD index of Barcelona city is 100, therefore neighborhoods with RFD index lower than 100 represent that their RFD index are below average of the city and the same logic goes with neighborhoods with RFD index higher or equall than 100. Today, Barcelona contains 73 different neighborhoods, to better illustrate the RFD index by neighborhoods, we plot this data onto a choropleth map. A choropleth map is a type of statistical thematic map that uses pseudocolor, i.e., color corresponding with an aggregate summary of a geographic characteristic within spatial enumeration units, such as population density or per-capita income.

Here we present two different choropleth map, the one on the left have neighborhoods colored from yellow to red according to their RFD index, as illustrated by the colorscale on top of the map. Choropleth map on the right uses two different colorscale to better differentiate neighborhoods with lower than average RFD index with neighborhoods with equall or higher than average RFD index. By placing the mouse cursor or clicking the neighborhood you can see the associated neighborhood name and its RFD index.

As we can observe, most of the neighborhoods in the north east or south part of the city seem to have RFD index lower than average and the highest RFD index neighborhoods are located in the north east part of the city. 49 neighborhoods have below average RFD index and 24 neighborhoods have equal or higher RFD index than average. The 3 highest RFD index neighborhoods are Pedralbes, les Tres Torres, and Sarrià, with RFD index of 248.8, 215.8, and 193.6 respectively. The 3 lowest RFD index neighborhoods are Ciutat Meridiana, la Marina del Prat Vermell, and Vallbona, with RFD index of 38.6, 40.0, and 40.9 respectively. These maps indicate that there is a substantial income inequality among the neighborhoods, we can check if this inequality has been increasing or decreasing over the past years by comparing with RFD index of the neighborhoods from prvious years.

The oldest dataset available in the repository is from year 2007, therefore we compare the RFD index of year 2007 with year 2017 to calculate the percentage changes of RFD index over the past 10 years. Choropleth map on the left below illustrates the RFD index with 2 different colorscales, choropleth map on the right illustrates the RFD percentage change over the last decade, negative and positive changes are also differentiated by 2 different colorscale.

The income distribution in 2007 is very similar to 2017. 50 neighborhoods have below average RFD index and 23 neighborhoods have equal or higher RFD index than average. The 3 highest RFD index neighborhoods are les Tres Torres, Pedralbes, and Sant Gervasi - Galvany, with RFD index of 215.3, 193.6, and 187.0 respectively. The 3 lowest RFD index neighborhoods are Baró de Viver, Can Peguera, and Vallbona, with RFD index of 44.5, 49.8, and 51.6 respectively. Some of the poorer neighborhoods had a positive RFD change such as Baró de Viver with a positive RFD change of 54.83%, and some of the richer neighborhoods had some minor negative RFD change such as Vallvidrera, el Tibidabo i les Planes, with a RFD change of -1.57%. However, overall, the inequality among different neighborhoods has clearly increased over the last decade. Most neighborhoods in the north east part of the city have experienced a decrease in RFD index. In 2017, most of the low income neighborhoods are even relatively poorer than 10 decades ago.

Besides RFD index dataset, we also have disposable household income per capita by neighborhood available in public, the oldest one is from year 2015 and the most recent one is from year 2019. Choropleth maps below indicates the disposable income of these two years.

The absolute value of disposable income in 2019 is clearly higher than in 2015, however, the overall distribution is basically the same as with the RFD index choropleth map of year 2017 and 2007. Most of the highest income neighborhoods are in the district of Sarrià-Sant Gervasi and most of the lowest income neighborhoods are in Nou barris.




2. Other demographic and relevant indicators in the city of Barcelona

In this section, we plot other demographic and relevant indicators. The firs two dataset we use is the Immigration registration rate (‰ inhabitants) and Birth rate (‰ inhabitants) of the city of Barcelona. These two rate is the relation between the number of immigration or birth registration in one year and the population of the same year per thousand inhabitants. Choropleth maps below plots these two rates for the most recent available year 2020.

Most of the neighborhoods have immigration rate between 30 to 50 ‰ inhabitants and birth rate between 4 to 8 ‰ inhabitants.

The other dataset we plot is the life expectancy rate by five years average. The most recent one is from year 2015 to 2019.
